Global Pea aphid distribution and climate change

Our project focuses on Species Distribution Models (SDMs) and their application to climate change by studying the pea aphid (Acyrthosiphon pisum), an important agricultural pest. The goal is to predict how the aphid’s global distribution will change due to shifting climatic conditions. we are using historical data from 1970 to 2024 and making future predictions for 2021-2040 and 2041-2060 based on three climate scenarios: SSP126 (low emissions), SSP245 (moderate emissions), and SSP370 (high emissions).

For the historical analysis, weconsider variables like soil quality, precipitation, temperature, elevation, wind speed, solar radiation, and humidity. For the future predictions, wefocus on precipitation of the driest month, mean temperature of the driest and wettest quarters, isothermality, and the mean diurnal range. These variables are essential for predicting how temperature and precipitation changes will affect the aphid’s range. We are using MaxEnt (Maximum Entropy) for SDM to model the relationship between the aphid’s distribution and environmental factors. The model will predict future distributions, providing maps of the aphid’s current and future range under different climate scenarios. Performance metrics like Area Under the Curve (AUC) and confusion matrices will evaluate the model’s accuracy.

The expected outcomes include identifying regions where the aphid’s range may expand or contract, helping to predict areas at risk of future aphid infestations. This will provide insights for pest management and agriculture, allowing for better planning in response to climate change impacts.
